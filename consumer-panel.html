BRAID Consumer Panel

Shaping public priorities for brain health research through meaningful engagement

Consumer Panel Highlights Public Priorities for Brain Health Research

The BRAID team recently held its first Consumer Panel engagement session, bringing together members of the public to discuss the future of brain health research, the concept of brain frailty, and the responsible use of artificial intelligence and federated data infrastructures.

About This Session

This was the SAIL Databank Consumer Panel, held as part of BRAID's Public Involvement and Engagement (PIE) programme. It aimed to co-design research approaches with patients and members of the public and featured discussion of brain health biomarkers, trust in AI-driven analytics, and governance for privacy-preserving research infrastructures. The Consumer Panel provided valuable feedback that will help shape future engagement activities, research design, and communication strategies.

Introducing Brain Frailty

During the meeting, the BRAID team presented its vision for advancing large-scale brain health research through privacy-preserving federated analytics. The project seeks to address long-standing challenges in neuroimaging research, including fragmented data access, inconsistent data recording, and limited opportunities for large-scale analysis. By enabling secure analysis across multiple trusted research environments, BRAID aims to support new approaches to understanding brain health and identifying markers of brain frailty.

The presentation highlighted how imaging-based measures of brain health, combined with routine healthcare information, could help improve prediction of health outcomes and support more personalised approaches to care.

What We Heard from the Consumer Panel

Public Awareness Gap

A recurring theme was the limited public awareness of brain health and brain frailty compared to more familiar concepts like BMI. Participants showed enthusiasm for raising awareness of brain ageing and prevention.

Clear Communication Essential

Panel members emphasised the importance of using accessible language for complex concepts. Alternative terminology, practical examples, and relatable explanations would improve understanding and engagement.

Prevention is Priority

Strong interest in lifestyle factors affecting brain health — alcohol, smoking, exercise, stress, mental health, head injuries, and lifelong learning. Greater education on long-term impacts needed.

Key Discussion Areas

Representation and Access

Participants questioned how findings from individuals undergoing clinical brain scans can be generalised to broader populations. They highlighted the need to consider underrepresented groups, including carers, neurodivergent individuals, and communities facing barriers to research participation.

Trust and Transparency

These emerged as key considerations. Participants expressed interest in understanding how data are used, how AI-driven analyses operate, and how privacy is protected when research is conducted across multiple organisations and data environments.

Recommendations from the Consumer Panel

The Consumer Panel provided several practical recommendations for future public involvement activities:

Education & Awareness

Develop accessible educational materials explaining brain frailty, brain age, and brain health using real-world examples.

Co-Design Activities

Involve public contributors in the design of future workshops, questionnaires, and engagement activities.

Expand Beyond Traditional Audiences

Work with community groups, schools, universities, charities, sports organisations, carers' networks, and older adult groups.

Active Inclusion

Ensure recruitment strategies actively reach underrepresented communities and underrepresented groups.

Flexible Participation

Consider offering both group and individual participation options to support inclusive involvement.

Emotional Support

Establish clear support and signposting processes for participants who may find discussions about brain health emotionally challenging.

Health Equity

Continue exploring how sex, gender, and health inequalities may influence brain health research and outcomes.

BRAID Public Involvement & Engagement Roadmap

Based on the Consumer Panel feedback, BRAID has developed a structured 10-month engagement plan to expand public involvement and co-design research priorities.

Months 1–2

Building Understanding & Refining Communication

Objectives:
  • Improve public understanding of brain frailty, brain age, and brain health
  • Address concerns raised during the first Consumer Panel
Activities:
  • Develop plain-language descriptions of key concepts
  • Create visual communication materials and FAQs
  • Test terminology with Consumer Panel members
  • Produce a "What We Heard" summary
Outputs:
  • Public-facing communications toolkit
  • Revised engagement materials
  • Consumer-informed glossary of terms
Months 2–5

Expanding Public Involvement & Diversity

Objectives:
  • Broaden participation beyond existing networks
  • Ensure diverse perspectives are represented
Activities:
  • Recruit through community groups, charities, and organisations
  • Targeted outreach to underrepresented communities
  • Hold workshops exploring brain health understanding, biomarkers, and acceptability
Outputs:
  • Expanded stakeholder network
  • Diversity and inclusion recruitment plan
  • Interim engagement report
Months 5–8

Co-Designing Trust, Governance & Research Priorities

Objectives:
  • Incorporate public perspectives into technical and ethical discussions
  • Understand expectations around data use and AI
Activities:
  • Workshops on trust and transparency in AI
  • Privacy-preserving federated research infrastructures
  • Data governance and public oversight
  • Co-design participant-facing information
Outputs:
  • Public recommendations for trustworthy AI
  • Co-designed information resources
  • Public values framework for BRAID
Months 8–10

Evaluation, Integration & Future Planning

Objectives:
  • Evaluate the impact of public involvement activities
  • Translate findings into recommendations for future programmes
Activities:
  • Assess participant experiences and understanding
  • Evaluate perceptions of trust and acceptability
  • Synthesize findings from all activities
  • Final Consumer Panel validation meeting
Outputs:
  • Final PIE Report
  • Public Priorities & Recommendations Framework
  • Lessons Learned document
  • Future engagement and sustainability plan

Looking Ahead

The feedback received during this first Consumer Panel session will play an important role in shaping BRAID's future engagement activities and research programme. The discussion reinforced the importance of public partnership in developing trustworthy, inclusive, and impactful brain health research.

As BRAID progresses, the team will continue working with patients, public contributors, researchers, and healthcare professionals to ensure that advances in brain imaging, artificial intelligence, and federated data analysis are developed in ways that align with public values and deliver meaningful benefits for society.

We would like to thank all Consumer Panel members for their thoughtful contributions, constructive feedback, and enthusiasm for the project. Their insights will help guide the next phase of BRAID's public involvement and engagement activities.
